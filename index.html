Explicit Modeling: Part II

Ask questions that are well situated. Too much freedom leads to paralysis.

5.2 Conjecture. The exterior angles of a pentagon, one choice made at each vertex, add up to four right angles.
5.3 Question. What is the sum of the exterior angles of a hexagon? What about a general n-gon? Can you find a way to build on our understanding from small values of n, to general values of n?

Remember to give them plenty of stuff that is not quite true.

Like the last slide.

Only open up when they are ready, and then tread lightly.

Take advantage of incomplete arguments!

The student can earn a conjecture instead of a theorem. Depending on the situation, you might ask the student to state it themselves before they sit down.

(Eventually) Ask questions with uncertain outcomes.

and then respect the outcomes.


For me, it is usually about making definitions. Define "polygon." Define "convex polygon." Define "point X is an interior point of" a polygon.

Be OPEN to student work.

I regularly get 15-18 solid student conjectures and questions to add to my task sequence each term.
